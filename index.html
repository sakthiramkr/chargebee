2023 · Data-Driven Decision Making

Using Customer Data to Unlock a Stalled Revenue Line

How aggregating signals across four data sources surfaced a product gap and changed the roadmap.

TrainerCentral's pipeline was growing but late-stage deals kept stalling — and no one had a systematic view of why. I built a signal aggregation engine pulling from four sources simultaneously: G2 and Capterra review patterns, Reddit community discussions, support ticket themes, and CRM lost-deal notes. The brief was simple: find what the data was consistently saying that no one was reading.

A clear pattern emerged across all four sources. Enterprise buyers — the segment we were actively trying to grow — were flagging SCORM compliance as a blocker. They needed to import existing training content built in standard authoring tools, and without SCORM support, content migration was impossible and deals couldn't progress. Every major competitor had it. The signal was strong enough across sources to quantify: multiple six-figure opportunities were stalled or lost against this single gap.

I compiled the findings into a business case: competitive analysis, cross-source signal volume, blocked pipeline estimate, and a phased rollout proposal. Presented it to the product team. SCORM was prioritised within one quarter and is now in beta — deployed first to the accounts that had flagged it directly.

2023–2024 · Usage Analytics × Feature Gating

Cutting Paid Churn in Half Without Touching the Price Tag

11% to 5% paid churn, 18% revenue lift in two quarters — through usage signals, not a pricing change.

Churn was concentrated in accounts that never reached a real activation moment in their first 30 days, and flat pricing meant power users and low-intent free trials looked identical on paper. Rather than wait for a pricing rebuild, I mined usage data and support ticket themes to find the specific feature-adoption gaps that predicted churn before it happened.

Three interventions came out of that analysis. In-product nudges pushed at-risk accounts toward the features most correlated with retention. Feature limits on the free tier converted low-intent signups into qualified trials instead of dead weight. Lifecycle email sequences were triggered by usage thresholds rather than calendar days, so the message landed when it was actually relevant.

Paid churn dropped from 11% to 5% and revenue grew 18% across two quarters — proof that monetization work doesn't always have to start with the price. Sometimes it starts with knowing which accounts are about to leave, and why.

Pricing Architecture · Designed, Pre-Launch
2025–2026 · Multi-Tier Pricing & Upmarket Packaging

Architecting a Multi-Tier Pricing Model for an Upmarket Shift

Feature-gated tiers, usage-based add-on pricing, and an enterprise framework — designed and cohort-validated ahead of rollout.

Flat pricing didn't reflect how differently a solo trainer and a 200-person training company use the product, and it gave enterprise buyers nothing to point to on SSO, encryption, or audit trails when security review came up. I designed a multi-tier structure — feature-gated Starter and Professional plans, with packaging boundaries set by where usage data showed the steepest jump in feature dependence — and cohort-tested a new mid-tier with existing customers to validate willingness to pay before committing to final caps.

Where consumption is genuinely variable and visible to the buyer — live workshop attendance — I priced it usage-based by attendee volume rather than folding it into a flat tier, so cost scales with the value a customer actually pulls from that feature. The core platform stayed flat-fee, since predictability matters more than precision for SMB and mid-market buyers comparing tools on a spreadsheet. That's the general rule I worked from: flat-fee where the buyer needs to budget with confidence, usage-based only where the cost driver is something the buyer can see and control.

Alongside the tiering, I scoped an enterprise packaging framework (SSO, field-level encryption, audit trails, advanced analytics) and rebuilt the positioning narrative from creator-focused tooling to infrastructure-grade platform value, so the pricing page and the security conversation finally say the same thing. This model is in final design ahead of rollout — what's documented here is the architecture and the reasoning behind it, not yet a shipped result.

Why the result made sense

Headline shift
"Training" vs "Course Creation"
"Course creation" positions the buyer as a content producer. "Training" positions them as a business owner — a higher-intent framing for the SMB and corporate segment we were targeting.
Pricing removal
Homepage is not a purchase page
Showing pricing before value is established invites comparison shopping and price anchoring. Moving it downstream lets the product make the case first.
Design layout
Visual hierarchy change

Learning applied
The test result validated a repositioning hypothesis, not just a copy preference. It gave internal justification for the broader messaging shift from "course platform" to "training platform" — which subsequently carried through to ad copy, email sequences, and sales collateral.
